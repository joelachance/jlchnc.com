# High Stakes

The stakes of software are changing. This person coined the term "plastic
software," which I saw through a David Kramer post on X and realized was a
great way of describing the current state of software.

I'm by no means suggesting that being able to easily create ideas you've had
for a long time is bad; however, I do think software that is used by the
masses and is the backbone to the infrastructure of the internet should be well
vetted, well built, and robust.

In most instances in my life when I've wanted to create something, it's either
been for myself or a small group of users. It's rarely been created out of the
necessity or the expectation that millions would use it. That's where most
ideas start anyway. I was a professional musician for a long time. For me,
music always started with the feeling of satisfaction internally. It never
started with the realization that somebody else might be satisfied from it
(even though that's a nice byproduct of creating something). Ideas are born out
of internal inspiration. It's incredibly rare for a product to be built knowing
a wide open market existed and would instantly mint millions. Those creators
started with a simple idea and the belief the idea needed to exist and having a
vision for what its existence would look like.

This should be obvious, but feels worth saying these days: stakes still exist
in software.
Right now, everyone has been discussing software as if it's trivial to build,
and that's not true.

Small projects of course can be trivial; they can break; they can look bad.
For software that supports personal
data, other businesses, and millions of users, you need hardened software.

You get this from a creator that has done this before and knows what pitfalls
can occur, and you get this from someone who has taken the time to test and use
their product. There is no free lunch.

AI will continue to improve, but there is still a strong requirement for skill
and thoughtfulness.

Stakes aren't just reliability, though. There's this almost formulaic pattern
you'll find on Twitter and amongst early-stage founders, which is to build
something until it functions and get people to try it.

I don't see anything wrong with this. I think this is a great pattern to follow
because it gives you a signal as to whether or not the idea is worth continuing
to pursue.

Once you find an idea worth pursuing, it's important to know that the way you
built your MVP isn't necessarily the way you should be building a maintainable
software product.

I think there's a possibility, a real possibility, that people who are vibe
coders, and I think this will continue to become more and more true, that this
group can deploy professional software, not plastic software.

I think a time is coming when dogfooding is of utmost importance. Knowing what
to change and when to change it against your vision and against user feedback
is incredibly important.

Consider the stakes of relationships. The same jokes might land over and over
and be successful. Your greatest hits might be good for years, but as
relationships develop, there's nuances.

There are times when there's a requirement to sit and be still and listen.
There are opportunities to make changes based on feedback that you hadn't heard
before. There's perspective shift. All of these amount to changes that end up
making a stronger relationship.

There's obvious parallels here, but the most important part is
the thoughtfulness that goes into creating. The thoughtfulness that goes into
actions and reflections and responses.

It's the essence of yourself that you put into a project or a relationship or a
company. Given that, in some ways the craft of software engineering is
changing, the stakes of relationships have never been more important. The
stakes of being yourself have never been more important.
